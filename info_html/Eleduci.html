Echinoderes leduci

Echinoderes leduci Grzelak and Sørensen, 202 2

Short description

with spines in middorsal position on segments 4, 6 and 8 and spines in lateroventral positions on segments 6 to 9. Tubes present in laterodorsal and ventrolateral positions on segment 2, lateroventral positions on segment 5, sublateral positi

ons on segment 8, and laterodorsal positions on segment 9. Sexually dimorphic tubes furthermore present in laterodorsal positions on segment 10 in males. Minute scales present on segments 2 to 10, but regular cuticular hairs absent throughout trunk.

cies can be distinguished from most other species by its complete lack of cuticular hairs. This trait is only shaped with Echinoderes dalzottoi and Meristoderes glaber.
Furthermore, having tubes on segment 2 in only laterodorsal and ventrolateral positions as in E. leduciis quite rare character, and only shared with two other Echinoderes species, i.e., E. daenerysae and E. higginsi. Neither of them can be confused with E. leduci though. Except for the dense cuticular hair covering observed in both species, E. daenerysae possesses only two middorsal spines on segments 6 and 8, while E. higginsi, although having the same number and arrangement of middorsal spines as E. leduci, is characterized by long, conspicuous spinous tergal extensions of segment 11 and lack of tubes on segment 9.

Distribution and habitat

known from Pahaua- and Honeycomb Canyon at the Hikurangi Range off New Zealand, on depths from 670 to 1013 m.
