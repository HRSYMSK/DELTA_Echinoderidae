The species has the short, well-spaced triangular tergal extensions that are typical for E. dujardinii species group members. Lateral terminal spines are acicular, and females have lateral terminal accessory spines, and with ventromedial female papillae formed by crescentic substructure and a tube on segment 8; similar ventrolateral female papillae might also be present on segment 7.

Key characters and similar species

Distinguishing species within the E. dujardinii species group can be really challenging. Species of the group are generally characterised by

glandular cell outlets type 2 in laterodorsal or midlateral positons on segments 8 and/or 9, sternal female papillae on certain segments from 6 to 8, short, well-spaced triangular tergal extensions, and the same spine pattern: middorsal spines on s

egments 4 to 8, lateroventral tubes on segments 2 and 5, lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, and laterodorsal spines on segment 10. The only variation regards lateral accessory spines on segment 8, that are present in E. dujardinii and E. gerardi only.

E. dujardinii group, E. pacificus shows most resemblance with the long spined species, i.e., species where the middorsal spines of segments 5 to 8 surpasses the posterior margin of their respective segments, and especially the middorsal spine of segment 8 surpasses its segment withmore than 40% percent of its length. Besides E. sublicarum these species include, E. kozloffiand E. pacificus. These three species are also characterized by the lack of glandular cell outlets type 2 on segment 9 – a character that is otherwise present in all other E. dujardinii group species, except the short spined E. songae.

Female specimens can furthermore be distinguished by their female papillae that are present on the sternal plates of segment 8 only (and perhaps 7, but never segment 6) to segments 6, 7 and 8 in E. kozloffi. Distinguishing E. sublicarum from E. pacificus is much more difficult, and currently the two species can only be distinguished by differences in trunk length (318-373 in E. pacificus vs. 204-272 µm in E. sublicarum), andby the abruptly tapering lateral terminal spines in E. pacificus.

The species is only known from its type locality in the saltwater marshes near Georgetown, North Carolina, where it lives inside hydrozoan colonies ( Eudendrium sp.).
