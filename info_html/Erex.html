Short description

Plump and very large species (so far the largest in its genus) belonging to the Echinoderes coulli

species group, which accommodates (mostly) intertidal species, characterized by shortage of spines and enlarged sieve plates. A short, flexible middorsal spine is present on segment 4, and lateroventral tubes are present on segment 5, very short, flexible

lateroventral spines on segments 6 and 7, and lateroventral tubes on segment 8. Laterodorsal tubes are also present on segment 10, but they are extremely short, and often impossible to see. Minute glandular cell outlets type 2 in the shape of fringed tubes are present in numerous positions on segments 2 to 8. Lateral terminal spines are extremely short – basically not lonmger than the short, triangular tergal extensions, and lateral terminal accessory spines are not present in females. Sieve plates are large and elongate.

Key characters and similar species

The general appearance of the species, and in particular the large, elongate sieve plate easily lead the observer to realize that this species belong to the E. coulli species group. Within this group, the spine/tube pattern of E. rex only resembles the pattern of E. ohtsukai, but the two species are very easily distinguished because E. ohtsukaihas rather long lateral terminal spines, whereas E. rex hardly has any. The extremely short lateral terminal spines generally make it very easy to distinguish E. rexfrom all other echinoderids.

Distribution and habitat

The species was described from subtidal mud at the Korean south coast (note in this context that it is rather uncommon to find subtidal species from the E. coulli species group), and it has subsequently been collected at different localities in Japan.
