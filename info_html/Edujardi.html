Echinoderes dujardinii

Echinoderes dujardinii Claparède, 1863

Short description

Species with short middorsal spines on segments 4 to 8, lateroventral tubes on segments 2 and 5, lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, lateral accessory tubes on segment 8, and laterodorsal tubes on segment 10. Middorsal spines on segments 7 and 8 might reach posterior margins of their respective segments, but they never extend beyond the margins. Minute glandular cell outlets type 2 in laterodorsal positions on segments 8 and 9. Tergal extensions of segment 11 short, pointed and well-spaced; sternal extensions short, with ventrolateral seta-like tuft of extended fringe tips. Females with lateral terminal accessory spines, and ventromedial female papillae resembling glandular cell outlets type 2 on segments 6 to 8; the intracuticular substructures of thepapillae arecrescenticon segment 6, and tubular on segments 7 and 8.

Key characters and similar species

Echinoderes dujardinii species group that includes species with the spine and tube pattern described above (except for the presence of lateral accessory tubes on segment 8, which are present in E. dujardinii and E. gerardi only), laterodorsal/midlateral glandular cell outlets type 2 on segments 8 and/or 9, short triangular tergal extensions, and females with female papillae onsome sternal plates. Within this species group only two species, E. dujardiniiand E. gerardi have lateral accessory tubes on segment 8, hence, this is the main character to distinguish these particular species from the rest.
E. dujardinii can only be distinguished from E. gerardiby the relative length of its middorsal spines. Whereas E. gerardi

has extremely short middorsal spines where none of them are in any way close to reach the posterior margin of their respective segments, the middorsal spines on segments 7 and 8 in E. dujardinii might actually reach the posterior margins of their respective segments.

Distribution and habitat

has been reported in numerous older contributions from several places in the world, but in particular from the Atlantic West European coast, and from the Mediterranean. A recent review of the species (the study is still ongoing) suggests though, that E. dujardiniiis restricted to the Atlantic West European coast, and that all Mediterranean reports of the species are in fact E. gerardi. The species has occasionally been reported from tidal and subtidal mud, but it also appears to be very common on macroalgae, which might in fact represent its primary habitat.
