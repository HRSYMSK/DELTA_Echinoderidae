Echinoderes landersi

Echinoderes landersi Grzelak and Sørensen, 202 2

Short description

with spines in middorsal position on segments 4 and 6, and in lateroventral positions on segments 6 to 9. Tubes present in subdorsal, laterodorsal, sublateral and ventrolateral positions on segment 2, lateroventral positions on segment 5, subdorsal, midlateral and lateral accessory positions on segment 8, and midlateral positions on segments 9 and 10.

iseasily distinguished from all other congeners by its combination of spines and tubes. The spine pattern on its dorsal side, with middorsal spines on segments 4 and 6, is already an uncommon feature within Echinoderes. This trait itself is shared only with E. astridae, E. bispinosus, E. uozumiiand E. dalzottoi. Thecharacter in combination with its tubes on segment 2 makes E. landersi unique among congeners. Abovementioned species possess either three pairs of tubes as in E. dalzottoior only one pair as in E. astridae, E. bispinosusand E. uozumii. Theref ore, having four pairs of tubes on segment 2, located in subdorsal, laterodorsal, sublateral and ventrolateral positions, is uncommon and found exclusively in E. landersi.
The three latter species furthermore differ from E. landersisp. nov. by being equipped with glandular cell outlets type 2; such outlets are absent in E. landersi. The regular hair covering found in E. landersisp. nov. also distinguishes it from E. dalzottoi sp. nov., whichis characterized by having a trunk cuticle with perforation sites only but nocuticular hairs.

E. landersican be easily distinguished from these species by other traits, among which the composition of segment 8 with tubes in subdorsal, midlateral and lateral accessory positions is the most important, as these are not present in any other species of Echinoderes.

Distribution and habitat

Hikurangi Margin off New Zealand, where it has been recorded fromthe continental slope and Pahaua Canyon, on depths from 7 28 to 1 121 m.
