Short description

Cigar-shaped species with indication of intracuticular midventral fissure on segment 2. The species belongs to the E. dujardinii species group, which is currently being revised. This revision might end up concluding that E. lanceolatusis synonymous with another species of the group. Also the distribution of glandular cell outlets type 2 is currently unc

ertain for the species. It is likely though, that outlets are present in laterodorsal positions on segment 8 and/or 9, and that female papillae are present on certain sternal plates. According to the original description the species hasmiddorsal spines on segments 4 to 8, lateroventral tubes on segments 2 and 5, lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, and laterodorsal spines on segment 10. Middorsal spines are relatively long (compared to other E. dujardinii species group members), and always extending beyond the posterior segment margin. The species has the short, well-spaced triangular tergal extensions, that are typical for E. dujardinii species group members. Lateral terminal spines are acicular, and females have lateral terminal accessory spines. Sieve plate small.

Key characters and similar species

Distinguishing species within the E. dujardinii

species group can be really challenging. Species of the group are generally characterised by 1) glandular cell outlets type 2 in laterodorsal or midlateral positons on segments 8 and/or 9, sternal female papillae on certain segments from 6 to 8, short, well-spaced triangular tergal extensions, and the same spine pattern: middorsal spines on segments 4 to 8, lateroventral tubes on segments 2 and 5, lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, and laterodorsal spines on segment 10. The only variation regards lateral accessory spines on segment 8, that are present in E. dujardinii and E. gerardi only. One of the important keys to identity some of the species seems to be the relative length of the middorsal spines. In the case of E. lanceolatus, the middorsal spines always protrude slightly beyond the posterior segment margins, opposite to the situation in the very similar specie E. aureus.
A rather distinct intracuticular fissure midventrally on segment 2 distinguishes E. aureus from other species within the species group, but such a fissure is also present in some (but not all) types of E. lanceolatus. Hence, for now, it is unclear whether or not the two species are synonymous.

Distribution and habitat

The species is described from the Korean island Jeju-do, and from localities along the Korean south coast, and at Dokdo Islands in the Japanese Sea. The populations at Jeju might in fact turn out to be E. aureus, but it is possible that the others represent a different species.
