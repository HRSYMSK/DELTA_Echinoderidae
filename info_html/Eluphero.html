Short description

Slender species with long, acicular tergal extensions, belonging to the E. spinifurca

species group. Middorsal spines are present on segments 4 to 8, ventrolateral tubes on segment 2, lateroventral tubes on segment 5, lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, and midlateral tubes on segment 10. All tubes are quite long and well-developed. Very small glandular cell outlets type 2 are present in subdorsal positions on segment 2, and in laterodorsal positions on segments 8 and 9. Lateral terminal spines are acicular; females with lateral terminal accessory spines and female papillae on sternal plates of segments 7 and 8.
Tergal extensions are long, slender and aciculate; trunk length/tergal extension length: 6.0 – 8.2 (average 7.2)%. Sieve plates small.

Key characters and similar species

The species belongs to the Echinoderes spinifurca species group that all share the same spine/tube pattern (see above) and are characterized by long, acicular tergal extensions, i.e., tergal extensions r

esembling needles rather than gradually tapering triangular tips. Presence/absence of glandular cell outlets type 2 varies within the group, but if present, they would always be at segments 2, 8 and 9. Species within the group are distinguished by the absence/presence of these outlets, and by differences in trunk length/tergal extension length ratio (=TL/TE ratio).

E. lupherorumhas glandular cell outlets type 2 on segments 2, 8 and 9, which makes it differ from E. spinifurca (not outlets at all) and E. sylviae (only outlets on segments 8 and 9). It is also easily distinguished from E. yamasakii that has very long tergal extensions, i.e., TL/TE ratio of 14.3% - twice as long as in E. lupherorum. The species shows most resemblance with E. kohniand E. microaperturus. The easiest way to distinguish it from E. kohni is by examining the pectinate fringe of the sternal plates of segment 10.
E. lupherorum just has a regular fringe, whereas the fringe tips in E. kohniare conspicuously large.
E. microaperturus haveslightly shorter tergal extensions (TL/TE ratio of 5.0% in E. microaperturus versus 7.2% in E. lupherorum), but otherwise the species mostly differ by differences in sensory spot distributions.
