Short description

Slender species with long, acicular tergal extensions, belonging to the E. spinifurca species group.
Middorsal spines are present on segments 4 to 8, ventrolateral tubes on segment 2, lateroventral tubes on segment 5, lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, and midlateral tubes on segment 10. All tubes are quite long and well-developed. Very small glandular cell outlets type 2 are present in subdorsal positions on segment 2, and in laterodorsal positions on segments 8 and 9.
Other noteworthy characters include conspicuously large fringe tips along the posterior margins of sternal plates of segment 10. Lateral terminal spines are acicular; females with lateral terminal accessory spines and female papillae on sternal plates of segments 7 and 8. Tergal extensions are long, slender and aciculate; trunk length/tergal extension length: 8.0 – 8.6 (average 8.3)%. Sieve plates small.

Key characters and similar species

The species belongs to the Echinoderes spinifurca species group that all share the same spine/tube pattern (see above) and are characterized by long, acicular tergal extensions, i.e., tergal extensions resembling needles rather than gradually tapering triangular tips. Presence/absence of glandular cell outlets type 2 varies within the group, but if present, they would always be at segments 2, 8 and 9.
Species within the group are distinguished by the absence/presence of these outlets, and by differences in trunk length/tergal extension length ratio (=TL/TE ratio).

Among the E. spinifurca species group members, E. kohnihas a unique character trait, i.e., conspicuously large fringe tips of its sternal plates of segment 10. Otherwise, E. kohnihas glandular cell outlets type 2 on segments 2, 8 and 9, which makes it differ from E. spinifurca (not outlets at all) and E. sylviae (only outlets on segments 8 and 9). It is also easily distinguished from E. yamasakii that has very long tergal extensions, i.e., TL/TE ratio of 14.3%, and from E. microaperturus that on the opposite has very short tergal extensions, TL/TE ratio of 5.0%.
E. kohniis most similar with E. lupherorum, but besides the above mentioned fringe tips on segment 10 and slightly longer tergal extensions (TL/TE ratio of 8.3% in E. kohni versus 7.2% in E. lupherorum), the species can be distinguished by differences in sensory spot distributions, e.g., E. kohnihas sublateral sensory spots on segment 1, which are absent in E. lupherorum (see page 61 in original description to have all differences listed).

Distribution and habitat

The species was described from muddy shell hash near the Friday Harbour Lab at San Juan Island, Washington State, and from Padilla Bay at the mainland. Prior to its description, M. Herranz collected the species from Bamfield, at Vancouver Island, British Columbia, Canada.
