Short description

Relatively plump species, belonging to the Echinoderes coulli species group. The species appears to have a middorsal spineon segment 4, but this spine is so short and delicate that it is difficult, and in some cases impossible, to observes.
Other very small spines are present in lateroventral positions on segment 6, and in sublateral positions of segment 7. Tubes are present in lateroventral positions of segment 5, in lateral accessory positions on segment 8, and in laterodorsal positions on segment 10. Tergal extensionsare short and triangular. Two conspicuous and significant character traits regard the sieve plates and theposterior segments. The sieve plates are very large as in other species of the Echinoderes coulli species group, but instead of the more common elongate, oval shape, the sieve plates of E. maxwelliare triangular. Another uncommon trait is segments 10 and 11 that can be retracted into segment 9. Lateral terminal spines are acicular and thin, and lateral terminal accessory spines are lacking in females.

Key characters and similar species

The general lack of spines (except for lateral terminal ones) combined with the large sieve plate, easily narrows the number of potential species down to those that are part of the Echinoderes coulli species group. Among these species, the number of potential species is further reduced by the triangular shape of the sieve plates and the ability to retract segments 10 and 11 into segment 9 – the latter trait is shared only with E. applicitus, E. rex and E. strii. The triangular sieve plate, on the other hand, is only shared with E. komatsui, hence the combination of the two characters is itself sufficient to recognize E. maxwelli. To easy identification even further, also the spine/tube pattern is unique for this species, hence, precise identification can be reached in several different ways.

Distribution and habitat

cies was described from an estuary near Cape Town, South Africa. Subsequently it has been found abundantly in the St. Lucia estuary, South Africa, and near Paje at Zanzibar, Tanzania.
