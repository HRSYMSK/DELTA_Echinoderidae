Short description

E. dujardinii species group, and has middorsal spines on segments 4 to 8, lateroventral tubes on segments 2 and 5, lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, and laterodorsal tubes on segment 10.
Midlateral glandular cell outlets type 2 present on segments 8 and 9.
Middorsal spines may reach, but never extend beyond the posterior segment margin. The species has the short, well-spaced triangular tergal extensions that are typical for E. dujardinii species group members. Lateral terminal spines are acicular, and females have lateral terminal accessory spines, and ventromedial female papillae resembling glandular cell outlets

type 2 on segments 6 to 8; intracuticular substructure of female papillae crescentic on segment 6, andtubular on segments 7 and 8.

Key characters and similar species

Distinguishing species within the E. dujardinii

species group can be really challenging. Species of the group are generally characterised by glandular cell outlets type 2 in laterodorsal or midlateral positons on segments 8 and/or 9, sternal female papillae on certain segments from 6 to 8, short, well-spaced triangular tergal extensions, and the same spine pattern: middorsal spines on segments 4 to 8, lateroventral tubes on segments 2 and 5, lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, and later odorsal spines on segment 10. The only variation regards lateral accessory spines on segment 8, that are present in E. dujardinii and E. gerardi only. One of the important keys to identity some of the species seems to be the relative length of the middorsal spines.

E. sensibilis belongs to the short spined species (i.e., species with middorsal spines notextending beyond their posterior segment margins), which easily distinguishesit from the long spined species E. kozloffi, E. pacificusand E. sublicarum. It is also distinguished from the two short spined species E. dujardiniiand E. gerardiby its lack of lateral accessory tubes on segment 8. The three species showing closest resemblance with E. sensibilisare E.
imperforatus, E. songae and E. chandrasekharai.
E. songae can unambiguously be distinguished by its lack of glandular cell outlets type 2 on segment 9, whereas theintracuticular structure of female papillae differ in E.
chandrasekharai ( E. sensibilis females:
segments 7 and 8:
papillae forming short intracuticular tubes; E.
chandrasekharai females: segments 7 and 8:
papillae forming a semicircle with protuberance).

Echinoderes sensibilis and E. imperforatusare so similar that they can basically only be distinguished by the more distinct cuticular perforation sites of cuticular hairs in E. sensibilis. The only other difference potential difference is the lateral terminal spines that tend to be longer in E. imperforatus, butthe ranges are still slightly overlapping (132– 168 μµm in E. sensibilis vs 164– 192 μµm in E. imperforatus). Higgins (1983) suggests that E. imperforatuscan be distinguished from other members of the species group by its complete lack of cuticular perforation sites.
However, although they are relatively indistinct, perforation sites are actually present and can be observed with LM, and without this diagnostic character, the species is basically identical with E. sensibilis, and it is fair to speculate if the two species are conspecific.

E. sensibilis and E. aureus overlaps in East Asia, but even though the two species resemble each other in some ways, they are also easily distinguished.
In the case of E. sensibilis, the middorsal spines are shorter than those in E. aureus, and it also lacks the partial midventral fissure in segment 2 that is present in E. aureus.
They furthermore differ by the dorsal distribution pattern of glandular cell outlets type 1: E. sensibilishas (as all other E. dujardinii species group members) subdorsal glandular cell outlets type 1 on segments 4 to 9, whereas E. aureushas paradorsal outlets on segments 4, 6, 8 and 9, and middorsal ones on segments 5 and 7.

Distribution and habitat
