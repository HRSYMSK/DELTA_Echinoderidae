Short description

Slender species with middorsal spines on segments 4 to 8, lateroventral tubes on segment 5, and lateroventral spines on segments 6 to 9, expect on segment 8 where the spine has been replaced to lateral accessory/sublateral positions. Laterodorsal tubes arealso present on segment 10, but they are very thin and may be hard to see. Glandular cell outlets type 2 are present on segment 2 in subdorsal and lateroventral positions, and on segment 8 (these are conspicuously large) in midlateral positions (not included in original description). Other noteworthy characters include the presence of a middorsal protuberance between segments 10 and 11, and the complete lack of cuticular hairs on segment 10. Lateral terminal spines acicular, and females with lateral terminal accessory spines. Sieve plate small.

Key characters and similar species

The species has a rather stereotypical spine pattern, but it is still quite easily recognized because of the laterally displaced spines on segment 8. This might sounds like a subtle character, but because they are spines (and not tubes), the displacement is very easy to see, and actually only shared with E. eximus and E. tchefouensis. However, the latter species have no lateroventral spines on segment 6 and 7, so they cannot be confused with E. bookhouti. The midlateral glandular cell outlets type 2 on segment 8 are usually also very easy to see, due to their considerable sizes.

Distribution and habitat

was described from North Carolina at the US East Coast, but has subsequently been reported from numerous localities in in the northern Gulf of Mexico, where it is extremely abundant.
