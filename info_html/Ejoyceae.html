Short description

Relatively plump species with middorsal spines on segments 4, 6, and 8 (spine on segment 8 nearly reaching beyond terminal segment), ventrolateral tubes on segment 2, lateroventral tubes on segment 5, and later oventral spines on segments 6 to 9. Laterodorsal tubes also present on segment 10, emerging through slit-like openings medially on segment, and not at posterior segment margin as in most other species.
Quite large glandular cell outlets type 2 are present in subdorsal positions on segment 2, midlateral positions on segment 6, and in sublateral positions onsegment 8. Tergal extensions are short and triangular, with fringed tips. Lateral terminal spines acicular; females with rather short and thinlateral terminal accessory spines. Sieve plate small.

Key characters and similar species

The species can be recognized by its combined spine/tube pattern and distribution of glandular cell outlets type 2. Especially the presence of glandular cell outlets type 2 inmidlateral positions of segment 6 is quite uncommon, compared to the much more common midlateral outlets on segment 5. When keying the species, it might appear to be close to E. bermudensis, but this is mainly because it remains uncertain whether this species has glandular cell outlets type 2, and hence is coded as uncertain for this character. The species are not similar though, and for instance, the tergal extensions in E. bermudensisare way longer and stronger than in E. joyceae. E. joyceaeis instead much more similar with Meristoderes elleae, and besides the partially developed tergosternal fissures on segment 2 in M. elleae, the only other prominent difference between the species is the presence of sublateral glandular cell outlets type 2 in M. elleae.

Distribution and habitat

The species lives in subtidal mud along the shelf of the northeastern Gulf of Mexico. It is never very abundant, but can nevertheless be found from Texas and eastwards to the Florida Panhandle.
