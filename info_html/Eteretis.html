Short description

belonging to the Echinoderes coulli species group, with peculiar trunk shape, due the extraordinary broad tergal plates on the anterior segments. In unmounted specimens, or specimens mounted for SEM, this would in lateral view look like the specimen is hunchbacked, whereas in LM, the specimen is very clearly broader around segments 3 to 6, and then tapering considerably towards the terminal segments.

Short middorsal spine present on segment 4 only; tubes in lateroventral positions on segments 5, short, flexible spines in sublateral positions on segments 6 and 7, tubes in sublateral positions of segment 8, and short, hook-shaped tubes in laterodorsal positions on segment 10. Minute glandular cell outlets type 2 presentin subdorsal, laterodorsal and lateroventral positions on segment 2, in sublateral positions on segment 3, in subdorsal and sublateral positions on segment 4, and insublateral positions on segments 5 to 8. Lateral terminal spinesare acicular, rather thin and long, short and stout, and lateral terminal accessory spines are absent in females. Sieve plates large and elongate.

Key characters and similar species

The easiest way to recognize the species, is by the peculiar outline of its hunchbacked trunk. Otherwise, the very reduced of spines ( except for terminal ones) combined with the large sieve plate, easily narrows the number of potential species down to those that are part of the Echinoderes coulli species group. Among these species, the spine/tube pattern of E. teretisis in itself enough to distinguish the species.

Distribution and habitat

was described from Sidney Harbour and Broken Bay north of Sidney, NSW, Australia, but it seems to be abundant in both subtidal and intertidal estuaries along the southern coast of New South Wales and into the southeast coast of Victoria.
